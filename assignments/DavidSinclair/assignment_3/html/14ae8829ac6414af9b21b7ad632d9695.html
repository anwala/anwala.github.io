INSIDE THAILAND
Progress of the Government’s Implementation of Anti-Corruption Laws
27 January 2018 (Readers 119)

 
Deputy Prime Minister Wissanu Krea-ngam gave a briefing on the progress of the Government’s implementation of anti-corruption laws at a "Meet-the-Press” event, held at Government House on the topic "Laws for the Future of Thailand.”
 
The Deputy Prime Minister said that many relevant agencies confirmed that the Licensing Facilitation Act, which was announced as law in July 2015, has been able to tackle corruption to a great extent.
 
This Act applies to the granting of all permissions or licensing, as well as all registrations or notifications, for which an application is required prior to doing any activity. In rendering licensing facilitation to the public, each government agency is required to prepare a licensing manual for the public.
 
The manual consists of the rules, procedures, and conditions for the submission of the application, work flow, and period of time for the granting of the license, as well as necessary documents to be attached with the application.
 
Another anti-corruption law is the Government Procurement and Supplies Management Act 2017, which replaced the procurement regulations of the Prime Minister’s Office. This law sets penalties for authorities who take bribes. It also extends the adoption of the state procurement process to cover state enterprises, public organizations, and independent organizations, as well.
 
As for the draft bill on offences concerning conflict of interest between personal gain and public benefit, it is being considered by the National Legislative Assembly. The new law prohibits officials from approving government projects, or withdrawing a case, for their own personal gain.
 
Both officials and members of the private sector are prohibited from interfering in the appointment or transfer of officials for their own benefit. The bill is intended to prevent officials from receiving bribes by using their official positions.
