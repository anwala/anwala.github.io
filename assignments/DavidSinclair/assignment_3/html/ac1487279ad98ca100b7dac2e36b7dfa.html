Can global government eradicate war?

Rbo Sutherland / Thompson Citizen

January 31, 2018 12:00 AM

Is global government, in the sense of a federal democratic republic of the world with an entrenched bill of rights, conceivable as a solution to the prospects of a global thermonuclear WWW III?  Yes.  

If the cause of war can be known, then war itself can be eradicated.  Hobbes put his finger on the cause of war when he correctly defined war as not only the act of fighting (hot wars) but the settled intention to engage in the same, when conflict cannot be resolved through discussion (cold wars).  The cause of war is anarchy.  Anarchy exists whenever human beings or nations try to live together without each surrendering their sovereignty.  It exists wherever human beings or nations rely on discussion alone.  In all those circumstances, the settled intention to engage in active fighting, if needed, exists.

The cause of war lies outside of human nature.  By nature, human beings need peace, socially and politically, if they are to fulfill their self-evidently true moral duty to seek that which is really good, to lead good human lives for themselves. Peace is indispensable to the normal development of human life.  It is war that is abnormal, detrimental to the normal development of human life.  

Peace is the ability to resolve conflict through justice and law.   

The cause of peace is therefore good government.  Fundamental disagreements cannot be avoided, but the resort to violence can be avoided.  Just as local problems are solved through local governments, just as regional problems are solved through regional governments, just as national problems are problems are solved through national governments, so too global problems such as war can be solved through a global government.

Not just any government will do.  Effective government is always the combination of legitimate authority with authorized force.  Without right, human beings are not governed; they are merely overpowered.  Without might, human beings are not governed; they are merely admonished.  

The moral authority of government, its legitimacy, derives in part from the fact that it has the consent of the governed.  It is constitutional government, the rule of law not persons.  The people consent to a constitution which creates the primary office of citizenship and set out the individual offices citizens may hold and the powers of those offices.  They do so implicitly when they formally accept a proposed constitution.  They do so implicitly when they participate in the process but decline to accept a particular constitutions or fail to leave the state when the constitution is adopted.  When they accept the benefits of government and their consent is deemed.  The people then participate in the formation of the laws by which they are governed.  Citizenship is not enough.  The suffrage must extend politically to all who are capable of giving their consent: all adult men and women, regardless of race, creed, orientation or religious belief. That is political justice: treating equals equally to the extent of their equality; in this case, their equal capacity for self-governance. Suffrage is not enough.  The politically free can be economically exploited.  A just government regulates the economy to serve interest of human beings and provides just laws to ensure human beings receive a decent, not minimal, standard of living, whether that be through programs maximizing full employment, income supplements or combinations of both.  This is economic justice: treating equals equally to the extent of their equality; in this case, their equality as human beings in need of some measure of wealth in order to discharge adequately their moral and political obligations.  It does not require an equality of results beyond that floor of a decent standard of living.  Economic justice recognizes that once a basic equality of economic conditions is established, equality demands treating unequals unequally to the extent of their inequality; in this case, those who contribute more to the production of wealth are entitled to a greater share of that wealth.  But this situation only arises when all are truly economically free by having a decent standard of living and political laws effectively prevent the distortion of the political process by economic sources of power.  

Authorized force is never violence. The executive and the legislative branches of government implement their authority through the judiciary.  Courts resolve conflicts and impose their just will through the police and sheriffs.  Violence is the unauthorized force.   

A federal democratic republic of the world with an entrenched bill of rights is within our grasp.  All that is required is to the nations of world to surrender their external sovereignty, their so-called right to make war with one another.  They would necessarily retain their internal sovereignty of their government in purely national matters.  It is no longer a utopian fantasy.  A world community, based on the recognition of a common human nature and the natural moral rights and obligations that flow thereafter, is rapidly forming.  International problems are rapidlyu expanding. The need for such an institution is substantial and pressing. It is not the creation of a perfect society, merely a healthy global society where conflict is resolved through law and not war.

It is only a question of prudence in timing.  Prudence involves the navigation between two extremes.  Western democracies successfully navigated similar problems with the extension of the franchise to non-propertied and uneducated whites, blacks and women in the later 19th and early 20th centuries.  One is the error of supposing peace can be established by world political institutions prior to the moral and intellectual changes needed to make those institutions sufficiently acceptable to enough people.  The other is the error of supposing the heart and mind of man must be completely ready before the necessary institutions can be initiated.  Both the institutionalists and the moralists fail to see that the process is interactive.  Human beings must be morally and intellectually ready for the institutions but only to some degree.  Once the institutions exist, they will condition the whole social environment and produce further moral and intellectual changes favourable to their operation.  The moralist underestimates the educative influence of political institutions.  The institutionalist does not pay enough attention to the psychological soil in which the institutions take root.  

Rob Sutherland, who contributes Spiritual Thoughts columns to the Nickel Belt News, is a graduate of University of Toronto and Osgoode Hall Law School He is a criminal defence lawyer with 30 years experience, a member of the bars of Ontario, Alberta, Newfoundland, Nova Scotia and Manitoba. He is a Senior Canadian Fellow at the Mortimer J. Adler Centre for the Studies of the Great Ideas, an American think-tank based in Chicago. He has published one book “Putting God on Trial: the biblical Book of Job,” a defence of God’s goodness in the face of his authorization of undeserved and unremitted evil in the life of Job and the world, which is taught at a number of Canadian, American and Indian universities and available through Amazon. He is writing a second book “Putting Jesus on Trial: the biblical Gospels of Matthew, Mark, Luke and John,” a defence of Jesus’ divinity.
